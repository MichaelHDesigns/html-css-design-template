AREAS OF EXPERTISE (AMONG OTHERS)

The bulk of the sections related to financial instruments have been harmonized between IFRS and US GAAP. IFRS has, in my opinion, a neater classification and so will be discussed below with the applicable Accounting Standard Codification (ASC) noted.

IAS 32 Financial Instruments: Presentation

(Covers several ASC standards)

This section, for the most part, defines financial instruments, assets and liabilities.

IAS 39 Financial Instruments: Recognition and Measurement

(Covers several ASC standards and is to replaced by IFRS 9)

This sections outlines the requirements for recognition and measurement of financial assets, financial liabilities and some contracts to buy or sell non-financial items. Financial assets and liabilities are defined in this section. Financial instruments are initially recognised when an entity becomes a party to the contractual provisions of the instrument, and are classified into various categories depending upon the type of instrument, which then determines the subsequent measurement of the instrument (typically amortised cost or fair value). IAS 39 requires financial assets to be classified in one of the following categories:

  • Financial assets at fair value through profit or loss
  • Available-for-sale financial assets
  • Loans and receivables
  • Held-to-maturity investments

IAS 39 recognises two classes of financial liabilities:

  • Financial liabilities at fair value through profit or loss
  • Other financial liabilities measured at amortised cost using the effective interest method

This section discusses, at length, many caveats related to financial instruments. For most of what investment companies trade in, the instruments will be initially measured at fair value with subsequent changes in fair value going through the statement of income. All disclosures related to this section have been moved to IFRS 7.

IFRS 7 Financial Instruments: Disclosures

(Covers several ASC standards)

Organizations are required to disclose information about the significance of financial instruments (as defined in IAS 32) to an entity and the nature of risks arising from those financial instruments both in quantitative and qualitative terms. This section describes the necessary presentations in the statement of financial position, statement of comprehensive income and notes to the financial statements. Significant time can be spent on the note disclosures for this accounting standard especially in relation to the credit, liquidity and market risk sections of the note.

IFRS 13/ASC 820 Fair Value Measurement

This section applies to other IFRS sections that require or permit the use of fair value. To that end, IFRS 13 does not discuss when to apply this section but instead define fair value, provides a single framework for measuring fair values and outlines necessary disclosures related to fair value measurements. One of the main themes of this standard is that there is a hierarchy of 3 levels of inputs in fair value measurements.

  • Level 1 inputs - Level 1 inputs are quoted prices in active markets for identical assets or liabilities that the entity can access at the measurement date.
  • Level 2 inputs - Level 2 inputs are inputs other than quoted market prices included within Level 1 that are observable for the asset or liability, either directly or indirectly.
  • Level 3 inputs - Level 3 inputs inputs are unobservable inputs for the asset or liability.

The objective of a fair value measurement is to estimate the price at which an orderly transaction to sell the asset or to transfer the liability would take place between market participants at the measurement date under current market conditions. Several disclosures are to be made including the fair values in each input level and a description of valuation methods for level 2 and 3 investments.
